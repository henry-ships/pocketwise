Key Metrics to Compare

Monthly Payment

This is the amount due each month. A lower monthly payment can help your cash flow, but it often comes at the cost of a longer term â€” which means more total interest. Compare this figure alongside total cost to see the full picture.

Total Interest

The total amount of interest you'll pay over the life of the loan. This is where the real difference between two loans becomes apparent. Even a 1-2% rate difference can translate to thousands of dollars over a multi-year loan. Our tool highlights which loan charges less total interest so you can see the lifetime impact immediately.

True APR (Annual Percentage Rate)

The APR accounts for both the interest rate and any upfront fees (like origination fees or points), expressed as a single annual rate. This is the most accurate apples-to-apples comparison metric. A loan advertising 7% with a 5% origination fee has a much higher true APR than a loan at 8.5% with no fees. Always compare APR, not just the stated interest rate.

Origination Fees

Origination fees are charged by lenders to cover processing costs, typically ranging from 1% to 8% of the loan amount. These fees are usually deducted from your loan proceeds before you receive them, meaning you get less cash but repay the full amount. Our calculator factors these fees into the true APR calculation so you can see their real impact on your borrowing cost.

Common Loan Comparison Mistakes

  • Only comparing monthly payments: A lower payment usually means a longer term and more total interest. Always check total cost alongside the monthly figure.
  • Ignoring fees: A "0% origination fee" loan at a slightly higher rate may cost less overall than a "low rate" loan with a 5% fee. The true APR reveals the real difference.
  • Comparing different loan amounts: If one lender offers $15,000 and another offers $20,000, the comparison isn't apples to apples. Match the amounts to what you actually need.
  • Forgetting about flexibility: Beyond the numbers, consider prepayment penalties, payment flexibility, and lender reputation. The cheapest loan isn't always the best if it locks you into rigid terms.
