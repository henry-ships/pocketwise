Average Net Worth by Age: Where Do You Stand?

Average Net Worth by Age: The Real Numbers and What They Mean for You

At some point, most of us wonder the same thing: Am I doing okay financially compared to other people my age? It's a fair question, and checking in on where you stand is a healthy financial habit — as long as you use the right benchmarks.

The problem is that a lot of the numbers floating around online are either outdated, cherry-picked, or presented in a way that makes everyone feel behind. This guide cuts through that noise. We'll look at the most reliable data available — the Federal Reserve's Survey of Consumer Finances (SCF), published every three years — break down what those numbers actually mean, and give you a practical framework for improving your own situation no matter where you're starting from.

One important note before we dive in: your net worth is a snapshot, not a verdict. It tells you where you are today, not where you're capable of going.

What Is Net Worth, and Why Does It Matter?

Net worth is the single cleanest measure of financial health available to you. It's simple arithmetic:

Net Worth = Total Assets − Total Liabilities

Your assets include everything you own that has monetary value: checking and savings account balances, investment and retirement accounts, real estate equity, vehicles, and any other property. Your liabilities are everything you owe: mortgage balance, car loans, student loans, credit card debt, and any other outstanding obligations.

The resulting number — positive or negative — is your net worth.

Why does this matter more than income? Because income is a flow, and net worth is a stock. You can earn a high salary and still have a negative net worth if you're spending everything and carrying significant debt. Conversely, someone with a modest income who has lived below their means for decades can accumulate substantial wealth. Net worth captures the full picture in a way that your paycheck simply can't.

Think of it this way: income tells you how fast water is flowing into a bucket. Net worth tells you how much water is actually in the bucket. Both matter, but only one of them can fund your retirement.

Average Net Worth by Age: Mean vs. Median (and Why Both Numbers Are on the Table)

When researchers report net worth data, they typically publish two figures: the mean (the mathematical average of all households) and the median (the exact midpoint where half of households fall above and half fall below). Understanding the difference is critical before you look at any number and judge yourself against it.

The table below draws from the most recent Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances. These figures reflect U.S. household net worth and are adjusted to reflect the 2022 survey cycle.

Age Group Median Net Worth Mean Net Worth
Under 35 $39,000 $183,500
35–44 $135,600 $549,600
45–54 $247,200 $975,800
55–64 $364,500 $1,566,900
65–74 $409,900 $1,794,600
75 and older $335,600 $1,624,100

Source: Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances, 2022

Those gaps between mean and median are enormous — and they're not a coincidence. The mean is being dragged upward by a relatively small number of extremely wealthy households. A single billionaire in the dataset can push the average up by millions while the typical household's situation remains unchanged.

This is why the median is the benchmark worth paying attention to. It tells you what a genuinely typical American household has accumulated — not what the average would be if you redistributed Jeff Bezos's net worth across all age groups.

A Few Things to Keep in Mind About These Numbers

Before you measure yourself against this table, a few important caveats:

  • These are household figures. A dual-income couple with two earners is counted as a single household. If you're single, comparing yourself to these numbers isn't perfectly apples-to-apples.
  • Cost of living varies enormously. A $250,000 net worth means something very different in rural Kansas than it does in San Francisco.
  • Retirement account balances are included. A significant portion of net worth for older age groups is locked up in 401(k)s and IRAs — money you can't spend today without penalties.
  • Home equity is the dominant asset for most Americans. Roughly two-thirds of American household net worth is tied up in real estate equity, which is illiquid unless you sell or borrow against it.

Use the table as orientation, not as a final grade.

Why the Median Is the Number That Actually Matters

Let's spend a minute on this because it genuinely changes how you should interpret any financial benchmark you come across.

Imagine ten people sitting in a coffee shop. Nine of them have net worths ranging from $50,000 to $200,000. The tenth is a tech founder worth $50 million. The average net worth of everyone in that coffee shop is now over $5 million. But does that tell you anything useful about the nine regular people? Not really.

This is called skewness, and wealth data is among the most skewed distributions that exist. The wealthiest 1% of U.S. households hold more wealth than the bottom 90% combined. When you average across a distribution that lopsided, the mean becomes almost meaningless as a benchmark for ordinary people.

The median, by contrast, gives you the number right in the middle. Fifty percent of households your age have more. Fifty percent have less. That's the comparison worth making.

So when a headline screams "Average American Retires With $1.2 Million!" treat it with extreme skepticism. That headline is almost certainly reporting the mean, and it's almost certainly making most readers feel catastrophically behind when the real comparison — the median — would paint a far more accurate (and often less alarming) picture.

The practical implication: if you're near the median for your age group, you're genuinely in the middle of the pack for American households. Not ahead, not wildly behind. Right in the thick of it.

How to Calculate Your Own Net Worth in 20 Minutes

Knowing the benchmarks is only useful once you know your own number. Here's a simple, honest process for calculating yours.

Step 1: List All Your Assets

Go through each category and write down the current value:

  • Cash and savings: Checking, savings, money market accounts, CDs, any cash you keep on hand
  • Investment accounts: Brokerage accounts, stocks, bonds, ETFs, mutual funds held outside of retirement accounts
  • Retirement accounts: 401(k), 403(b), IRA, Roth IRA, pension present value (if applicable)
  • Real estate: Current market value of your home or any investment properties — not what you paid for them, what they'd sell for today
  • Vehicles: Current market value (use Kelley Blue Book or a similar tool, and be honest — cars depreciate fast)
  • Other assets: Business ownership equity, valuable collectibles, jewelry, life insurance cash value

Add all of these up. That's your total asset value.

Step 2: List All Your Liabilities

  • Mortgage balance: How much you still owe on your home, not the home's value
  • Car loans: Outstanding balances on any vehicle financing
  • Student loans: Federal and private, combined current balances
  • Credit card debt: Total outstanding balances across all cards
  • Personal loans: Any unsecured debt
  • Other: Medical debt, family loans you're obligated to repay, tax liabilities

Add these up. That's your total liabilities.

Step 3: Subtract

Assets minus liabilities equals your net worth. If the number is negative, you're not alone — many people under 35 have negative net worths, largely due to student loans and limited time to build assets. A negative net worth is a starting point, not a permanent state.

Once you have your number, write it down along with the date. Recalculate quarterly or at least annually. Watching it move — even slowly — in the right direction is genuinely motivating.

What the Numbers Look Like at Each Life Stage

The table tells you the benchmarks, but the context behind each age group helps explain why those numbers are what they are — and what typically drives the biggest jumps.

Under 35: Building the Foundation

A median net worth of $39,000 in this age group often reflects the reality of entry-level salaries, student loan repayment, and the early stages of homeownership (or renting while saving for a down payment). The good news is that time is your most powerful asset here. Every dollar invested in your twenties has decades to compound.

The priorities at this stage: eliminate high-interest debt, build a 3–6 month emergency fund, and start contributing to any employer-matched retirement plan. Don't skip the match — it's an immediate 50–100% return on your contribution.

35–44: Accumulation Starts in Earnest

Median net worth jumps to $135,600 in this bracket — a nearly 3.5x increase from the under-35 group. This often reflects home equity accumulation, more substantial retirement balances, and higher earning years. It's also when financial mistakes from earlier years start to either resolve or compound in the wrong direction.

If you bought a home in your late twenties or early thirties, the equity buildup is doing significant work here. If you've been consistently investing even modest amounts in your 401(k), compound growth is starting to become visible.

45–54: The High-Earning Years

The 45–54 age group shows a median of $247,200. For many households, this is the peak earning decade. Ideally, debt is declining, children are becoming more financially independent, and retirement accounts have grown significantly from years of contributions and market returns.

This is also when the retirement conversation shifts from abstract to urgent. If you're behind on savings, these years offer the highest catch-up potential — both because income is typically at its peak and because 401(k) catch-up contribution limits kick in at age 50.

55–64: The Final Push Before Retirement

Median net worth of $364,500 in this bracket often masks wide variation. Some households in this group are genuinely retirement-ready. Others are facing the reality that they haven't saved enough and have a decade or less to close the gap.

This is when detailed retirement planning becomes non-negotiable — not vague aspirations, but actual math: what will you need monthly, what will Social Security cover, and what does your savings need to look like to fill the gap?

65–74: Transition and Distribution

The peak median net worth bracket at $409,900 reflects households that have typically paid off most of their mortgage, drawn down other debts, and continued investing through their early sixties. Many are newly retired or in the transition phase.

A key shift happens here: the goal is no longer purely accumulation but thoughtful distribution. How you draw down assets matters almost as much as how you accumulated them.

75 and Older: Drawdown Phase

The slight dip to $335,600 in this final group reflects households actively drawing down savings for living expenses and healthcare. This is expected and by design — that's what retirement assets are for.

What to Do If You're Behind — And Why It's Not as Dire as It Feels

If you looked at the table and your number is well below the median for your age, take a breath. Being behind on a benchmark isn't a personal failure. It's data. And data is useful because it tells you where to point your energy.

First: Understand Why You're Behind

There's a meaningful difference between being behind because of circumstances outside your control (health crisis, job loss, divorce, supporting family members) and being behind because of sustained overspending. The former requires a compassionate reset; the latter requires honest habit change. Neither is shameful — but diagnosing which situation you're in determines the right next steps.

The Highest-Impact Moves, in Order

If you want a prioritized framework for financial decision-making, the financial order of operations is the clearest guide we know of. But here's the condensed version for someone focused on building net worth who feels behind:

  1. Stop the bleeding first. High-interest debt — especially credit cards charging 20%+ — is a net worth destroyer. No investment strategy reliably beats a guaranteed 22% return from paying off a credit card. This comes before anything else.
  2. Capture free money. If your employer offers a 401(k) match and you're not contributing enough to get the full match, you're leaving part of your compensation on the table. This is free money. Get it.
  3. Build a real emergency fund. Without 3–6 months of expenses in liquid savings, any unexpected event — a job loss, a medical bill, a car repair — sends you back into debt. The emergency fund is what makes every other financial plan sustainable.
  4. Increase income if the math requires it. Sometimes the gap between where you are and where you need to be is too large to close through cutting expenses alone. A career move, a skill upgrade, or a side income stream may be part of the answer.
  5. Invest consistently, even if imperfectly. The best investment strategy is the one you actually stick to. If you're not sure where to start, the investing basics guide covers the fundamentals without the jargon.

The Compounding Math Is on Your Side More Than You Think

Here's the thing about feeling behind: even modest consistent investing has a powerful trajectory. Someone who starts investing $400 a month at age 35 — with an average annual return of 7% — will have approximately $485,000 by age 65. That's not retirement-ready for everyone, but it's a meaningful foundation built from a realistic monthly contribution.

If you want to run your own numbers, the investment return calculator lets you model different contribution levels, timeframes, and return assumptions so you can see exactly how your decisions play out over decades.

The point isn't that everyone will get to the same place. It's that the trajectory is more forgiving than the current snapshot suggests, and starting — or restarting — is always worth doing.

Social Security Changes the Calculus

One variable that often gets overlooked when people calculate how "behind" they are: Social Security. For a typical American worker, Social Security will replace 40% or more of pre-retirement income. That's a guaranteed, inflation-adjusted income stream that doesn't require you to have a large investment portfolio to fund it. Your net worth target at retirement is much lower if you factor in the income Social Security will provide — and most people don't account for this when they're panicking about their savings rate.

You can check your projected Social Security benefit anytime at ssa.gov by creating a my Social Security account. It takes about five minutes and gives you a clearer picture of your actual retirement income gap.

Practical Strategies That Actually Move the Number

Knowing your net worth is step one. Moving it in the right direction is the ongoing work. Here are the levers with the most impact:

Automate Everything You Can

The single most effective thing most people can do for their net worth is to remove human decision-making from the equation as much as possible. Automatic 401(k) contributions, automatic IRA transfers, automatic extra mortgage payments — these eliminate the monthly negotiation with yourself about whether to invest or spend.

Attack the Right Debt

Not all debt is equally damaging. A 3% mortgage on an appreciating asset is categorically different from a 24% credit card balance on consumer spending. Focus aggressive paydown energy on high-interest consumer debt. Mortgage debt — especially at low rates — is often fine to pay off on schedule while directing extra cash to investments instead.

Revisit Your Housing Situation Honestly

For most Americans, home equity represents their largest single asset. But housing is also where significant wealth destruction happens — through buying more house than necessary, moving frequently (and resetting mortgage amortization each time), or cash-out refinancing for non-investment purposes. Your housing decisions have outsized impact on net worth accumulation.

Get Your Asset Allocation Right for Your Age

A 28-year-old and a 62-year-old should not be invested the same way. Growth-oriented portfolios make sense when you have decades to weather volatility. As retirement approaches, gradually shifting toward a more balanced mix protects against sequence-of-returns risk — the danger of a major market downturn right as you start drawing down your savings.

Minimize the Drag of Fees and Taxes

Investment fees compound just like returns do — in the wrong direction. An expense ratio of 1% versus 0.05% might sound trivial, but over 30 years it can consume a significant portion of your potential returns. Similarly, being thoughtful about which investments go in tax-advantaged accounts versus taxable accounts (tax location) can meaningfully improve after-tax net worth over time.
