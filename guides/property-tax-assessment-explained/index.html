How Property Tax Assessments Work (and How to Appeal Yours)

What Is a Property Tax Assessment (and Why Should You Care)?

Your property tax bill doesn't come out of thin air. Every year — or every few years, depending on where you live — a local government official called an assessor estimates the value of your home. That estimated value becomes the foundation for your property tax bill. Get assessed too high, and you're quietly overpaying hundreds or even thousands of dollars annually. Get it right, and you're paying your fair share and not a cent more.

Here's the thing most homeowners don't realize: assessments are wrong surprisingly often. Local assessors handle thousands of properties at once. They rely on mass appraisal models, neighborhood sales data, and sometimes outdated information. A finished basement that was never permitted. A bathroom count that hasn't been updated in 15 years. A declining neighborhood that still carries 2021 valuations. These errors happen all the time — and unless you push back, you'll keep paying the inflated bill.

Understanding how property tax assessments work isn't just a curiosity exercise. It's a legitimate way to protect your household budget, especially as home values have swung dramatically over the past few years. This guide walks you through the entire process: how assessments are calculated, what drives the numbers, how to read your assessment notice, and — most importantly — how to file an appeal that actually has a shot at succeeding.

How Property Tax Assessments Are Actually Calculated

Most people assume their property tax bill is simply a percentage of what their home is worth. That's close, but the mechanics have a few more moving parts.

Assessed Value vs. Market Value

Your home has a market value — roughly what a buyer would pay for it today in an open sale. It also has an assessed value, which is the number your local government uses to calculate your tax bill. In some states, assessed value equals market value. In others, it's a set percentage of market value, called the assessment ratio.

For example: If your home's market value is $400,000 and your state uses an 80% assessment ratio, your assessed value is $320,000. Your tax bill is then calculated on that $320,000 — not the full $400,000.

From there, your local government applies a mill rate (also called a millage rate) to your assessed value. One mill equals $1 per $1,000 of assessed value. So if your assessed value is $320,000 and your mill rate is 20 mills, your property tax bill is $6,400 per year.

The formula looks like this:

Assessed Value × (Mill Rate ÷ 1,000) = Annual Property Tax

How Assessors Determine Value

Assessors typically use one of three approaches — or a combination of all three:

  • Sales comparison approach: The most common method for residential properties. The assessor looks at recent sales of comparable homes (comps) in your area and adjusts for differences like square footage, lot size, age, and condition.
  • Cost approach: Estimates what it would cost to rebuild the structure from scratch, then subtracts depreciation and adds land value. Often used for newer or unique properties where sales data is thin.
  • Income approach: Used primarily for rental and commercial properties. Based on the income the property generates, capitalized into a present value.

For most single-family homeowners, the sales comparison approach is what matters. And because assessors are working from public records and bulk data — not walking through your home with a tape measure — errors in square footage, room counts, condition ratings, or property features are common.

Assessment Cycles: When Does Your Value Update?

This varies a lot by jurisdiction. Some counties reassess every year. Others reassess every two, three, or even five years. A few states reassess only when a property sells. This timing matters because it can create wide gaps between your assessed value and actual market value — sometimes in your favor, sometimes not.

In hot real estate markets, an infrequent assessment cycle can temporarily shield homeowners from huge tax increases. But it also means that when reassessment finally happens, the jump can be jarring. In cooling markets, an outdated (high) assessed value can mean you're paying taxes based on a value your home no longer carries.

Property Tax Rates by State: What Homeowners Are Actually Paying

Property tax rates vary enormously across the country — and the difference isn't trivial. A homeowner in New Jersey pays nearly ten times more in effective property taxes than one in Hawaii. Where you live matters a great deal when it comes to your annual bill.

A few things stand out from this data. First, a low effective rate doesn't always mean a low bill — California's rate is modest, but median home values are sky-high. Second, states like Illinois use a fractional assessment ratio, meaning your assessed value is only a fraction of market value, but the mill rate is correspondingly higher to compensate. The math usually evens out.

Understanding your state's specific rules is the first step to knowing whether your assessment is fair. The Lincoln Institute of Land Policy's 50-State Property Tax Comparison is one of the most reliable resources for digging into your state's specifics.

How to Read Your Assessment Notice (and Spot Red Flags)

When your assessment notice arrives in the mail — often in late winter or early spring — most homeowners glance at the number, wince, and file it away. That's a mistake. Your assessment notice is a document worth reading carefully, because the details it contains are the foundation of any successful appeal.

What the Notice Contains

A standard assessment notice will include:

  • Your property's assessed value (and sometimes a separate land value and improvement value)
  • The tax year the assessment applies to
  • Your property's physical description — square footage, bedroom/bathroom count, lot size, year built
  • The deadline to file an appeal if you disagree
  • Instructions on how to access comparable sales data the assessor used

Five Red Flags That Suggest Your Assessment Is Wrong

1. The property details are inaccurate. Check the square footage, number of bathrooms, garage size, and lot dimensions against your records. A single extra bathroom in the assessor's database can add $10,000–$20,000 to your assessed value.

2. Your assessed value jumped more than market appreciation warrants. If nearby sales don't support a 15% jump in values but your assessment went up 15%, that's worth questioning.

3. Your effective tax rate is meaningfully higher than your neighbors'. If two nearly identical homes on your street have very different assessed values, that's a comparability argument you can make in an appeal.

4. Your home has issues that reduce its market value. Foundation problems, a dated kitchen, deferred maintenance, backing up to a highway — these things reduce what a buyer would pay, but assessors often use models that don't fully account for condition.

5. You recently purchased the home below the assessed value. A recent arm's-length sale is powerful evidence of market value. If the assessor's number is higher than what you paid, that's an immediate appeal candidate.

How to Appeal Your Property Tax Assessment: A Step-by-Step Walkthrough

Property tax appeals have a reputation for being complicated and intimidating. In practice, most residential appeals are relatively straightforward — especially at the first level. Here's how to approach it.

Step 1: Know Your Deadline

This is non-negotiable. Every jurisdiction has an appeal deadline, and missing it means waiting another full year (at minimum). Deadlines are printed on your assessment notice. In most states, you have 30 to 90 days from the date the assessment notice is mailed. Mark the calendar the day the notice arrives.

Step 2: Research Comparable Sales

The heart of any residential appeal is comparable sales data — what did similar homes in your area actually sell for? You're looking for homes that are close to yours in size, age, location, and condition that sold within the past 6–12 months.

Good sources for comp data:

  • Your county assessor's public database (often free online)
  • Zillow, Redfin, or Realtor.com's "recently sold" filters
  • Your county recorder's property transfer records
  • A real estate agent who can pull MLS data for you (many will do this for free as a goodwill gesture)

Let's say your home was assessed at $450,000. You find four recent sales of comparable homes within half a mile — all between $390,000 and $415,000. That's your evidence. Document each comp with the address, sale date, sale price, and key features. The gap between your assessment ($450,000) and the average of your comps (~$405,000) is the core of your argument.

Step 3: Check the Assessor's Property Record for Errors

Request a copy of the assessor's property record card. This document contains everything the assessor knows about your property — or thinks they know. Look for:

  • Square footage discrepancies (especially if your home was measured years ago)
  • Extra bathrooms, fireplaces, or finished spaces that don't exist
  • Wrong year built (affects depreciation calculations)
  • Incorrect lot size
  • No credit for obvious condition issues

One homeowner in suburban Chicago found that the assessor had recorded his home as having a full finished basement. His basement was unfinished utility space. That single error accounted for nearly $18,000 in assessed value — and correcting it reduced his annual tax bill by about $540.

Step 4: File the Appeal

Most first-level appeals are filed with your local Board of Review (sometimes called the Board of Equalization or Assessment Review Board). The process is usually administrative — not a formal court proceeding. In many counties, you can file online.

Your appeal submission should include:

  • A completed appeal form (from your assessor's website)
  • Your evidence: comp sales data, property record corrections, photos if relevant
  • A brief written argument (one to two pages is plenty) explaining why your assessed value is too high and what you believe the correct value should be

You don't need a lawyer for a first-level appeal. You do need organized, factual evidence. Emotion doesn't move assessors — data does.

Step 5: Attend Your Hearing (If Required)

Some boards hold informal hearings where you present your case to a reviewer. Others make decisions on the paperwork alone. If you have a hearing:

  • Be concise. You'll often have 5–10 minutes.
  • Lead with your strongest argument — usually comparable sales.
  • Bring printed copies of everything for the board.
  • Stay factual and respectful. The reviewer isn't your adversary; they're doing a job.

Step 6: Escalate if Needed

If the Board of Review denies your appeal or doesn't reduce the assessment enough, you can typically escalate to a state-level board or take the matter to tax court. At this level, hiring a property tax attorney or a licensed appraiser often makes sense — especially if the dollar amount justifies it. Many property tax attorneys work on contingency, taking a percentage of your first year's savings.

What Are Your Odds?

Better than you might think. According to data from the National Taxpayers Union Foundation, homeowners who appeal their property tax assessments are successful (meaning they receive at least some reduction) in 30–60% of cases at the informal or first-level review stage. In some jurisdictions — particularly in Illinois, New York, and New Jersey — the success rates are even higher because the assessment process has historically been less precise.

The key variable isn't the jurisdiction — it's whether you come prepared. Homeowners who file appeals with documented comparable sales and corrected property data win at dramatically higher rates than those who appeal on gut feeling alone.

Other Ways to Reduce Your Property Tax Bill

Appealing your assessment is the most direct lever — but it's not the only one. Here are a few others worth knowing:

Homestead Exemptions

Most states offer a homestead exemption that reduces the taxable value of a primary residence. In Texas, for example, the standard homestead exemption removes $100,000 from your home's appraised value before taxes are calculated. In Florida, it's up to $50,000. These exemptions are typically not automatic — you have to apply. If you've owned your home for years and never filed, check whether you've been leaving money on the table.

Senior, Veteran, and Disability Exemptions

Many jurisdictions offer additional exemptions or circuit breaker programs for seniors on fixed incomes, disabled homeowners, and veterans. These can be substantial — in some cases eliminating property taxes entirely for qualifying households. Check your county assessor's website for a full list of available exemptions.

Agricultural and Conservation Designations

If you own rural land or a larger parcel, look into whether your property qualifies for agricultural, conservation easement, or greenbelt designations that carry significantly lower tax rates. These programs vary widely by state but can produce major savings for qualifying properties.

The Bigger Financial Picture: Property Taxes and Your Home's Value

Property taxes are one of the most persistent costs of homeownership — they don't disappear when your mortgage does. For many retirees who've paid off their homes, property taxes become the largest annual housing expense they face. That makes understanding and managing your assessment not just a one-time exercise but an ongoing financial habit.

When you're evaluating whether to buy in a particular area, the effective property tax rate should factor into your calculations just as much as the purchase price. A $350,000 home in New Jersey can easily generate $7,000–$8,000 in annual property taxes, while a similar home in Colorado might carry a $2,000 bill. Over 10 years, that difference exceeds $50,000 — money that could have gone toward retirement, your kids' education, or simply staying liquid.

It's also worth noting how property taxes interact with your overall tax picture. If you itemize deductions, you can deduct up to $10,000 in state and local taxes (including property taxes) under current federal law. For many homeowners in high-tax states, this cap has meaningfully reduced the federal offset they used to receive, making local property tax management even more important.

The bottom line: your assessment is not a fixed fact. It's an estimate made by a busy government office working from imperfect data. You have the right to challenge it, the tools to do it yourself, and a reasonable shot at winning. An hour or two of research and a well-prepared appeal filing could save you hundreds of dollars every year for as long as you own the home.
